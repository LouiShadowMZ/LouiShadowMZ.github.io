Chapter 31

THE MISSING HEARING AID

A woman for whom I had done horary charts in the past telephoned to say that she was missing a hearing aid and was unable to find it. She had only recently begun using hearing aids and had spent several thousand dollars on them. The modern technology was amazing; the hearing aids were quite small and were easy to misplace for that reason. Her horary question was this: “Where is my missing hearing aid?” It was asked at 1:01 p.m. EDT on 29 July 2015 at 41N17, 73W02 (chart 33). At the time, my astrology software was set to Placidus houses, and I didn’t bother to switch to Regiomontanus.

The querent had gone to the gym and from there to her office. After a while at her workplace, she noticed that one of her hearing aids was missing. She went out to the parking lot to look for it in her car, and she retraced her steps from the car to the office. She called the gym, but they could not find the missing hearing aid. Finally, at the end of her lunch break, after not finding the missing item, she called me to consult the stars.

It is a Mercury day during a Venus hour. According to Dorotheus of Sidon, a first-century Hellenistic astrologer, Venus is the day ruler of the Water triplicity. The Scorpio Ascendant, in the via combusta, belongs to the Water triplicity. The via combust Ascendant may mean that she had the misfortune of losing her hearing aid in an obscured location. In addition, Venus is closely sextile the Ascendant.

Mars rules the Scorpio Ascendant and signifies the querent. In addition, Mars is almuten of the 1st house. (The almuten of a point in the zodiac is the planet that has the most essential dignity at that point.)

Mars also rules and is almuten of the 2nd house cusp of possessions and thus signifies the missing hearing aid. The fact that Mars rules both the querent and the missing item suggests that she will find it.

Sagittarius is intercepted in the 2nd house (Placidus houses), making Jupiter a co-ruler of the hearing aid. Jupiter occupies the public 10th, an angular house, which suggests that the hearing aid may be near or at her office and easy to find.

Saturn in Scorpio on the cusp of the 2nd house suggests that Saturn may also be a co-ruler of the hearing aid. Saturn is disposed by Mars, and Mars (the querent) and Saturn are mutually applying to a favorable trine, suggesting that she will recover the missing item. Since Saturn rules the 3rd house (vehicles, local travel) and Mars rules the 6th (hygiene), I suggested that she look carefully in her vehicle (3rd house) and in the bathroom at her office (a 6th house association). Since Mars occupies the 9th (a long-distance travel house), the car as a means of transport seemed the more likely possibility. Furthermore, Mars in Cancer in the 9th house is disposed by the Moon, which is applying to Pluto in the 3rd house of local travel.

The Moon co-rules the querent, and the Moon and Pluto are applying mutually to a conjunction in the 3rd house of cars. In modern astrology, Pluto is a co-ruler of Scorpio and could potentially rule both the Ascendant and the 2nd house cusp. Traditional astrologers would never use Pluto in this way and would probably gasp apoplectically at any suggestion to do so.

More significantly, the Moon in Capricorn co-rules the querent and is disposed by Saturn in Scorpio. Saturn, in turn, is disposed by Mars (significator of the querent and the missing item) in Cancer, and Mars in Cancer is disposed by the Moon. This closed loop of dispositors links the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 9th houses, again suggesting that the missing hearing aid may be in the querent’s car. This is further suggested by the Ascendant in the via combusta, which may refer to the automobile’s combustion engine. Sometimes astrological symbolism can be quite literal.

Finally, the Moon (co-ruler of the querent) will oppose Mars (ruler of the 2nd house) and then sextile Saturn on the 2nd house cusp before leaving its sign. In addition, Mars (Ascendant ruler) will trine Saturn (3rd house ruler, the car) in five degrees and the 2nd house cusp in six degrees, showing a reunion of the querent with the missing item.

A few hours later, the querent telephoned to say that she had gone out to her car again during her afternoon break and found the missing item in a crease in the fabric where the seat cushion meets the adjustable back of the driver’s seat. She hadn’t seen it the first time she looked in her car during her lunch break.

The chart was a bit difficult to interpret, but the preponderance of indications suggested she would find it in the car, which turned out to be the case.

Which House Rules Automobiles?

There is a controversy in the horary literature about which astrological house rules cars. Horary astrologer Alphee Lavoie, for example, feels that the 2nd house signifies all the querent’s movable goods and therefore should be used as the significator of a lost or stolen automobile. In civil law, personal possessions are called “movable property,” meaning anything you own that is movable from one location to another. If something can be moved, it can be lost, misplaced, or stolen.

Olivia Barclay disagrees with Lavoie’s position and feels that automobiles are ruled by the 3rd house (rather than by the 2nd house of movable property), because cars are made for short trips and local travel. By a similar logic, taverns are assigned to the 5th house of fun and merriment rather than to the 4th of tenements and landed property. To prove her point, she cites a horary chart about a honey-colored 1937 Bentley in which the 3rd house gives a much more compelling description of the car than does the 2nd house. Unfortunately, her logic is flawed, because the querent asks whether he will have enough money to buy the car of his dreams. This is clearly a 2nd house question about finances, and there is no reason to assume, as Barclay does, that the 2nd house in this horary refers to a car. She concludes that the 3rd house signifies the Bentley and shows that the 2nd house does not, but it is obvious from the question that the 2nd house refers to the money needed to buy a car rather than the automobile itself. I tend to agree with Barclay that, in most horary charts, the 3rd house signifies cars as well as other means of local transportation.

I have been unable to find any traditional examples of horary questions about a coach, carriage, or cart used for transportation. There may be one buried in the ancient astrological texts that are now being translated. Lilly talks about ships at sea, but he uses the 1st house to represent the ship.

John Gadbury (1627–1704), a contemporary of William Lilly, wrote about horaries concerning the safety of a ship at sea (a large ocean-going vehicle). The ancient Arabic and Latin astrologers assigned ships to the 9th house of sea voyages, but Gadbury cites William Lilly that a judgment about the ship’s safety should be made by studying the 1st house as representative of the ocean vessel.

Lilly writes about the subject in Christian Astrology in chapter 26, “Of a Ship, and whatever are in her, Her Safety or Destruction”:

The Ancients do put this question to those concerning the 9th house, and I conceive for no other reason, than because it must be granted, that all Ships are made for travel and journeys: however, in regard the most part of the judgment concerning its safety or ruin is derived from the Ascendant and his Lord, and the Moon, I thought fit to place this judgment as belonging to the 1st house. Generally, the Sign ascending and the Moon are Significators of the Ship, and what goods are in her. (CA 157)

Note that here, Lilly stresses that “the most part of the judgment concerning its safety or ruin is derived from the Ascendant and his Lord, and the Moon.” In other words, Lilly’s focus is on the safety or ruin of a ship rather than on the ship itself, which the Ancients assigned to the 9th house. Interestingly, when Lilly discusses the various parts of the ship, he assigns them to the twelve signs of the zodiac rather than the twelve astrological houses.

Some two thousand years ago, Hellenistic astrologers assigned the life and vitality of the native to the Ascendant and 1st house of the natal chart. They used the metaphor of a human life as a sea voyage, and they assigned the helm, which steers the ship, to the Ascendant. Thus, Lilly is within the Hellenistic tradition when he assigns the safety and “life” of a ship to the 1st house and the Ascendant, which steers the ship on its journey.

Lilly’s chapter on the safety of ships follows shortly after his discussion of “whether one absent be dead or alive”:

If a Question be demanded of one absent in a general way, and the Querent hath no relation to the party; then the 1st house, the Lord of that house and the Moon shall signify the absent party; the Lord of the 8th House or Planet posited in the House or within five degrees of the Cusp of the 8th House shall shew his death or its quality. (CA 151)

Lilly is clearly drawing an analogy between an absent person and an absent ship when making horary judgments about their safety or destruction.

In Lilly’s time, the equivalent to a contemporary car would have been the person’s horse, which was used as a means of local transport. Lilly utilizes the 12th house to represent horses even though they can be considered movable personal property. The Trojan Horse of Homeric fame would probably be assigned to the 12th house as a type of secret enemy, even though it was the movable property of the Greek army commanded by Odysseus. When the steam locomotive came into use, Victorian society called it an “iron horse,” but no astrologer to my knowledge has assigned steam engines to the 12th house. Modern astrologers frequently assign airplanes to the 9th house, though airplanes can be considered the modern equivalent of ancient seagoing vessels used for long-
distance travel.

Perhaps the key to the matter of house assignment in horary is that there are no absolutes. Assigning an object to a given house depends on the role the object plays in the querent’s life and in the particular question, as well as on the querent’s relationship to the object.
