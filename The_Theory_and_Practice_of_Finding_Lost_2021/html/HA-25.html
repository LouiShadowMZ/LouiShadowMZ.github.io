Chapter 25

LILLY: WHERE IS THE ABSENT BROTHER?

Lilly writes about a querent who wanted to know whether his absent brother was dead or alive:

In November of 1645, a Citizen of London being gone into the West of England, and no news for many weeks had where he was, his own Brother with great importunity moved me to give my judgment concerning these particulars:

1. If living or dead? if dead, whether killed by soldiers? for at this time our miserable kingdom was full of soldiery.

2. If living, when he should hear of him? And where he was?

3. When he would come home?

The Ascendant doth here represent the shape and form of him that asked the Question, with consideration had to Saturn, Lord of the Sign; and as both the Sign ascending and Lord thereof are of a dry quality and nature, so was the Querent, lean, spare of body, and a real Saturnine man, &c. (CA 196–197)

Lilly begins by making sure the chart is radical and fit to judge. The Ascendant accurately describes the querent, so it looks like a valid chart (Chart 24). Lilly did not mention the fact that it is a Venus day during a Mars hour, nor did he work with dwads (12th parts). In this chart, the dwad of the Ascendant lies in Virgo—the sign on the 8th house cusp—which is fitting, as the querent is concerned about the possibility that his missing brother might be dead. In addition, the ruler of the Virgo dwad is Mercury, which occupies the 9th house of long-distance travel and foreign lands.

The querent’s brother is shown by the 3rd house, with Taurus on the cusp, making Venus the missing brother’s significator. The cusp of the 3rd becomes the brother’s derived Ascendant. Lilly explains that he is not using the Moon in this chart to describe the querent or his brother because the Moon does not form an applying aspect to the significator of either party.

For as much as Venus, significatrix of the Quesited, is no manner of way afflicted either by Mercury who is Lord of the 8th in the figure, or by Mars who is Lord of the eighth as to the ascendant of the quesited, and that the separation of the Moon was good, viz., from a dexter trine of Jupiter, and her next application to a conjunction of the Sun, upon the Cusp of the Medium Coeli, I judged the absent Brother was alive and had no manner of casualty happened unto him, but was in good health. Having judged the man to be alive there needs no proceeding to judgment of the rest of the first Query. (CA 197)

Lilly identifies the Lords of Death, of which there are two in this chart: the ruler of the radical 8th house of death and the ruler of the brother’s derived house of death, which is the 8th from the 3rd, or the radical 10th. Virgo on the cusp of the radical 8th makes Mercury a ruler of death for the querent, and Scorpio on the cusp of the 10th identifies Mars as a ruler of death of the 3rd-house brother. Either ruler of death is capable of killing the absent brother, but the good news is that neither significator of death afflicts the brother’s ruler Venus. Some astrologers might argue that only the ruler of the brother’s derived 8th house would indicate his death, but Lilly accepts the querent’s 8th house ruler of death as a potential killer as well.

Lilly then turns his attention to the Moon, which is a general signifier of fugitives and a mediator of the action of the other planets in the chart. He notes that the Moon has separated from a favorable trine with Jupiter (Table 12). Separating aspects have perfected in the past and indicate what happened prior to the horary question. Benefic Jupiter in Cancer is in its exaltation, making its trine even more favorable. The Moon has also previously conjoined Mercury, which is rather weak, being peregrine (without any essential dignity) and occupying the cadent 9th house.

The next aspect to be formed by the Moon will be a conjunction with the Sun at the Midheaven. Lilly feels that the future aspect to which the Moon is applying within orb confirms his judgment that the brother is alive and also indicates that nothing bad has happened to him in the recent past (Moon trine Jupiter) and that he is in good health (Moon applying to conjoin the Sun, a general signifier of vitality).

Lilly ignores the Moon’s final aspect, a conjunction with Mars at the very end of Scorpio, probably because Mars (the brother’s derived 8th house ruler of death) is not within orb of the Moon at the time of the question. (See Table 13 for the orbs used by Lilly in the seventeenth century and those used by Iranian astrologer Al-Biruni in the eleventh century. Astrologers should feel free to experiment with either set of orbs in their horary practice.) Here is the math. Lilly gives the Moon an orb of 12.5 degrees, and he gives Mars an orb of 7.5 degrees. To judge whether the aspect is within orb, you add the orb of the Moon to that of Mars and divide by 2: 12.5 (Moon) + 7.5 (Mars) = 20 degrees, which divided by 2 is 10 degrees, which represents the maximum distance that planets can be separated to be within orb of the aspect. In this chart, Mars at 29o Scorpio 06' is more than 13 degrees away from the Moon at 15o 52' Scorpio, and thus the conjunction is not within orb.

He hath since confidently affirmed, that about the very moment of time, viz. about four, a Carrier came casually where he was, and informed him his Brother was in health and living. (CA 198)

By modern computer calculations, transiting Venus perfected the trine to transiting Saturn at 11:29 a.m. GMT, shortly before the horary question was asked and about five hours earlier than Lilly calculated it to perfect, given the ephemeris of his times. Venus rules the 3rd house (the missing) brother, and Saturn rules the Ascendant (the querent). Venus is advancing at a faster than average speed, and retrograde Saturn is moving in apparent backward motion. Lilly interpreted the perfection of the trine between Venus and a retrograde Saturn so close to the time of the question (he believed it occurred shortly after he asked the question) as meaning that the querent would “very suddenly” receive news about his brother.

Each visible planet has a certain diameter when we look at it in the sky. Astronomers call the width of a visible planet its “apparent size” or “angular diameter.” Apparent size is measured in degrees, minutes, and seconds and simply means how big the planet appears when we look at it in the sky. Keep in mind that a circle contains 360 degrees. The apparent size of a planet will vary with its distance from the Earth and its location in its orbit around the Sun. Table 14 is a list of the visible planets with their range of apparent sizes.

The table of apparent sizes reveals that the largest diameter of Venus as seen from the Earth is 63 seconds of arc (a little more than 1’ of arc), and the largest diameter of Saturn is almost 20 seconds of arc. If the two planets were just touching side by side, the total length from the beginning of one planet to the end of the other would be 63” + 20” = 83”. Half of 83” is 41.5”, which is the maximum distance the planets can be apart and still maintain a bodily aspect between them (Figure 6). Thus, if the modern ephemeris is correct, Venus was separating from a trine to Saturn at the time of the horary question and Lilly’s interpretation of the chart is based on a miscalculation of the positions of Venus and Saturn. What a difference a minute of arc can make!

Lilly’s Orbs and Moieties

Lilly used his table of orbs to determine whether two planets were in aspect. As first discussed in Chapter 6 of this book, Lilly called a half part of each orb its moiety. For example, in Lilly’s table, the orb of Saturn is 10o 00', so the moiety of Saturn is half that number, or 5o 00'. Similarly, the orb of Venus is 8o 00', so the moiety of Venus is half that number, or 4o 00'. Thus, if Venus and Saturn lie within 9o (the sum of their moieties, i.e., 4o for Venus + 5o for Saturn = 9o), they are within orb of a platick aspect with each other (CA 107). For example, Venus at 10o Taurus forms a trine with Saturn at 18o Virgo because they are 8 degrees away from a perfect trine and lie within the sum of their moieties.

Void of Course (VOC) According to Lilly

Lilly used the concept of moieties to decide whether the Moon or any other planet was void of course (VOC), which he defined like this:

A planet is void of course when he is separated from a Planet, nor doth forthwith, during his being in that Sign, apply to any other. (CA 112)

This most commonly applies to the void-of-course Moon, which symbolizes the general flow of events surrounding the question, and

you shall seldom see a business go handsomely forward when she is so. (CA 112)

When the Moon enters the aspect void, nothing much happens to advance the querent’s interests; little happens to alter the status quo. While it is void of course, the Moon is traveling through an aspect vacuum, and it is the aspects that provide the power that drives the chart. Nothing happens when the Moon is applying to nothing. To use a modern metaphor, a VOC Moon is like a car spinning its wheels, going nowhere fast.

In his list of aphorisms, Lilly writes:

Generally consider the state of the Moon, for if she be void of course there’s no great hopes of the Question propounded, that it shall be effected; yet if she be in Cancer, Taurus, Sagittarius or Pisces, your fear may be less, for then she is not much impedited by being void of course. (CA 299)

Note that being void of course should not be interpreted in isolation but must be considered in the context of the entire chart. For example, despite being void, the Moon can do well in Cancer (domicile of the Moon, exaltation of Jupiter), Taurus (exaltation of the Moon, domicile of Venus), Sagittarius (domicile of Jupiter), and Pisces (domicile of Jupiter, exaltation of Venus). Of course, Jupiter and Venus are the two classical benefics that indicate good fortune.

Lilly probably borrowed his ideas about the void-of-course Moon from Bonatti, who wrote:

Consider, Whether the Moon be void of course? For that signifies that the thing enquired after shall scarce ever come to a good end, and not without much labour, sorrow, and trouble, unless the Lord of the Ascendant or significator of the thing, shall be in very good condition, and then it may be hindered, but not wholly frustrated; yet ’tis a good time then for drinking, bathing, feasting, &c., and to use ointments for taking away of hair, especially if she be in Scorpio. … Consider, Whether the Moon be in Cancer, Taurus, Sagittarius, or Pisces; for it signifies good in the business, although she be joined to the Infortunes and not to the Fortunes; nor does she, being void of course, prejudice so much in those places as elsewhere, provided she be not Combust, for then they will advantage her little or nothing. (Bonatti 1676, 30)

In general, the void-of-course Moon suggests that efforts directed toward accomplishing a goal are likely to be futile or ineffective. The VOC Moon can be interpreted as a slowing down, inaction, or an impediment to the realization of the matter inquired about. Under a void-of-course Moon, the business can be accomplished only with great effort and difficulty, if at all. As Lilly points out, a void-of-course Moon poses less of a problem when Luna occupies the signs of her major dignities (Cancer or Taurus) or the signs of Jupiter (Sagittarius or Pisces), the greater benefic. In addition, the behavior of the Moon during the void period carries forward the nature of the most recent aspect that Luna perfected before going void.

Applying Lilly’s definition, the Moon, during its stay in any sign, must be separated from another planet and, as it moves forward in the sign, must be more distant from the next planet that it might aspect by more than the sum of its moiety plus the moiety of the next aspectable planet. Lilly’s definition differs from the modern version of void of course, which states that the Moon must not perfect any aspect before leaving its sign. Lilly says that the Moon must not apply to aspect another planet to be considered void of course. It is important to note that Lilly is referring only to aspects involving the seven visible planets of antiquity when judging void-of-course status.

Does it make a difference whether the Moon is void at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end of a sign? The following remarks about the VOC Moon take into account some comments from Wroskopos’s blog (posted 19 February 2010), who attended a workshop of Deborah Houlding.

A VOC Moon at the beginning of a sign suggests that the situation is in its very initial stages, and there will be inaction, inactivity, or delay in initiating the course of action.

A VOC Moon in the middle of a sign means that the situation has come to a standstill, or it is beset by delays, inactivity, and idling until things get going again. For example, Wade Caves reports a horary in which the querent wants to know whether he will complete all the requirements on time to be able to get a grant to study at college (Caves, episode 18). In this horary chart, the Moon has just gone void of course at 6 degrees in its sign. Regarding the question, the querent’s application process has been delayed because his high school transcript did not arrive at the university in a timely manner. The querent is beset by a period of waiting and inactivity before he can find out whether he will receive the desired assistance.

A VOC Moon at the end of a sign typically implies that “you shall seldom see a business go handsomely forward” (CA 112). The matter may come to naught. The result inquired about may not manifest. Actions taken may prove to be futile or ineffective. Nothing significant regarding the question may take place. Any result achieved will only be accomplished slowly and with great effort, or with shame or loss. The matter may have already progressed to the point at which there is nothing the querent can do to affect the outcome.

Lilly gives an example in which the Moon at the end of a sign would be considered void of course by the modern definition but which he did not regard as VOC because the Moon applied to an opposition to the Sun. On 27 June 1646 at 7:33:56 a.m. LMT in London (Chart 26), a woman asked Lilly if she would marry the gentleman she desired (CA 385). The Moon lies at 27o 17' Sagittarius and will perfect no aspect before leaving its sign. However, the Moon applies to oppose the Sun at 5o 30' Cancer and will perfect that opposition in 8o 12', which is well within the sum of the moieties of the Moon and the Sun (6o 15' + 8o 30' = 14o 45' orb for the Sun-Moon aspect).

Lilly also says that he found “the Moon separated from void of course, and applying to the opposition of the Sun” (CA 386). The Moon came within 14o 45' of opposing the Sun when it reached 20o 45' Sagittarius; just prior to that, the Moon was void of course. In other words, from the moment the Moon left its opposition to Mercury (Moon at 16o 23' Sagittarius) and until the time the Moon reached 20o 45' Sagittarius, she was void of course.

This same example is noteworthy because Lilly bases much of his interpretation on the false belief (based on the ephemeris of his epoch) that Jupiter was applying to sextile Saturn. By modern computer calculations, Jupiter was in fact separating from a sextile to Saturn. Lilly’s chart for the horary question did not accord with the scientific reality of the planetary orbits as we understand them today. Was Lilly’s success in this case based on a lucky guess, accurate intuition, the suggestibility of his client, or some type of placebo effect? What is clear is that the horary principles Lilly used to delineate the chart were applied to inaccurate data, yet he counseled his client in such a way to bring about the desired outcome.

Dwads (12th Parts) and the Absent Brother

Recently I’ve been experimenting with dodecatemoria (Greek: ), or dwads (12th parts) in horary charts. The basic idea is that each 30-degree sign is divided into 12 equal parts of 2.5 degrees, each of which is a reflection of an entire sign. The first 2.5-degree part has the same nature as its sign, the second part belongs to the next sign in zodiacal order, and so on for the remaining parts. There are two ways of sequencing the dodecatemoria. The Hellenistic method starts the sequence at the beginning of the sign, as does the Jyotish divisional chart of dwadasamsa, hence the abbreviation dwad for a 12th part. The Babylonian method, found in Paulus of Alexandria, begins the sequence from the degree in question, and some authors reserve the name dodecatemorion for the Babylonian method. In this book, I follow the Hellenistic and Hindu method of calculating dwads.

In the “absent brother” chart (chart 27), the dwad (12th part) of the Ascendant lies at 5o 24’ Virgo. Hellenistic astrologers concerned themselves mainly with the zodiacal sign of the 12th part, any planets in that sign, and the placement of the ruler of the sign of the 12th part. The symbolism of these factors should match the querent’s concerns and current situation.

In chart 27, the specific degree of the 12th part of the Ascendant lies in the Regiomontanus 7th house, which signifies a direction to the west of the querent. Virgo (the sign of the 12th part of the Ascendant) is on the cusp of the 8th house of death. The ruler of the 12th part is Mercury, which occupies Scorpio in the 9th house of travel. Clearly, the querent is concerned about the possible death of someone who is traveling at a distance.

The Dwad (12th Part) and the Dispositor of the Moon

The sign of the 12th part of the Moon can help to determine whether a lost object will be found. Observations from the astrological literature about the Moon and its dwad in horary charts include the following:

• If the sign of the 12th part of the Moon is on the Ascendant or the Midheaven of the horary chart, the missing thing will usually be found.

  • If the Sun occupies the sign of the Moon’s 12th part (or if the Sun occupies the same sign as the Moon’s dispositor), the missing object will come to light.
  • Finally, if the Moon applies by major aspect to its own dispositor, that which is missing is likely to be found.

    In Lilly’s chart of the missing brother, the dispositor of the Scorpio Moon is Mars, which lies in the same sign as the Sun, so the missing brother will come to light. In addition, the Moon will conjoin its dispositor, Mars, just before Luna leaves the sign of Scorpio to enter Sagittarius.

    A note on 12th parts, or dwads: The Babylonians used the 12th parts at least as far back as the fifth century BCE. Masha’allah describes their use in interrogational astrology in his essay “On Hidden Things.” Since I started trying Masha’allah’s method, I have found 12th parts (especially that of the Ascendant) to be extremely helpful in horary charts. Many software programs will calculate the 12th parts, and a web search for “dodecatemoria calculator” will list sites that calculate these parts online.
